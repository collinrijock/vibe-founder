## Part 2) Customers + distribution (how value reaches people)

**Initial snippet:** *A product that doesn’t reach customers is a hobby.*  
**Question:** *How do customers discover you, try you, buy you, and come back?*

### A high-level execution system (principles → actions)

#### 1) Put “service to the customer” above “profit” (profit is the result)
- **Principle:** If you don’t serve customers exceptionally well, distribution gets harder and more expensive over time. ([Ogilvy on Advertising](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1614/?highlight=57601), [My Life and Work: Henry Ford](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2198/?location=00:50:06))
- **Action items:**
  - Write a 1-sentence “who we serve + what we do for them” statement and use it to judge marketing, pricing, and features.
  - Make 1 measurable customer promise (speed, reliability, outcome) and track it weekly.

#### 2) Obsess over the front line (customer experience is the strategy)
- **Principle:** Founders win by hating bureaucracy/complexity and celebrating the front line—the people closest to customers. ([John Bogle / Founder’s Mentality](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/1998/?location=01:08:05))
- **Action items:**
  - Founder time allocation rule: spend consistent time where real customer friction lives (support inbox, sales calls, deliveries, onboarding).
  - Maintain a short “Top 10 customer frictions” list; kill 1 per week.

#### 3) Pick *one* primary distribution strategy and go deep
- **Principle:** “One distribution strategy usually dominates all others.” Depth beats scattered activity. ([Peter Thiel](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2237/?location=00:41:58), plus “stay laser-focused on what matters” from Jacobs’ maxims: [How to Make a Few Billion Dollars](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1351/?highlight=43471))
- **Action items:**
  - Choose your primary engine (examples: outbound sales, partnerships, content, retail location, app store, referrals).
  - Define the single metric that proves it works (e.g., CAC payback, demos/week → close rate, foot traffic → conversion).
  - Defer secondary channels until the primary is repeatable.

#### 4) Build trust like it’s a product feature
- **Principle:** Honor guarantees in the customer’s favor; reliability compounds word-of-mouth. ([How to Be Rich — Getty’s rules](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/2334/?highlight=86745))
- **Action items:**
  - Add a clear guarantee or risk-reversal (even a small one) and make it easy to claim.
  - Tighten response times (speed often *is* the differentiator).

#### 5) Create “believers,” not just buyers
- **Principle:** The best distribution includes a cult-like following—customers who recruit other customers. ([Arnold and Me](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1370/?highlight=45024))
- **Action items:**
  - Define 1–2 identity traits your best customers share (what they’re proud to be).
  - Ritualize it: language, community touchpoints, consistent “signature” experience.

#### 6) Execute with fast loops: do → measure → adjust (don’t over-plan)
- **Principle:** Don’t let planning get in the way of doing; win by many small advances and frequent course-correction. ([Bloomberg by Bloomberg](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1428/?highlight=48362), [Paul Graham’s Essays Part 2](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2309/?location=00:40:03))
- **Action items:**
  - Run weekly distribution experiments (1 change at a time).
  - Keep a simple scorecard: leads → trials → purchases → repeat/referrals, and fix the biggest leak first.

#### 7) Treat distribution as an operating system: “execution is everything”
- **Principle:** Find “holes” in how the industry works; lean execution becomes culture. ([Direct From Dell](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/2819/?highlight=88612))
- **Action items:**
  - Write your “how we sell/deliver” playbook as soon as something works (scripts, steps, templates).
  - Standardize the repeatable parts; keep the human parts (trust-building) high-touch.
