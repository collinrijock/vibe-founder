Part 1) Product / service (the value delivered)
Initial snippet: Everything starts with the thing you do for the customer. If you start with the product/service and take it seriously, the rest of the business tends to form around it (pricing, operations, marketing, hiring). Prioritize service; profit is a result, not the purpose (Henry Ford (Today and Tomorrow), Ogilvy on Advertising).

Question posed: What problem do you solve, for whom, and why is your solution meaningfully better?

A high-level execution system (principles → actions)
1) Anchor on “service first,” not “profit first”
Principle: If you prioritize profit over serving the customer, you deserve to fail (Ogilvy on Advertising). The “central aim” is more/better goods & services to more people (How to Be Rich).
Actions:
Write a one-sentence service promise (what you do for the customer) and keep it above the profit goal in your internal docs (Ogilvy on Advertising).
Make “customer delight” a daily operating priority (not a slogan) (The Essays of Warren Buffett).
2) Start in a domain you can understand end-to-end
Principle: Choose a field you know and understand; “know what goes into making what you’re selling” (How to Be Rich, Authentic).
Actions:
Learn the full chain: how it’s made, delivered, supported, and why it fails.
If you can’t explain the product simply and confidently, you’re not ready to scale it (Authentic).
3) Different is not enough—be better in a way customers feel
Principle: Offer something they can’t get elsewhere—style + substance (Against the Odds). Great companies are built around a “secret” outsiders don’t see (Zero to One).
Actions:
Define your “only we can do this” advantage in one sentence (your secret).
Ship one flagship use-case where your advantage is obvious (don’t spread it thin).
4) Set “unrelentingly high standards” and protect the customer experience
Principle: One bad customer experience can undo hundreds of good ones; “it has to be perfect” (Working with Jeff Bezos).
Actions:
Pick 1–3 non-negotiable quality bars (speed, reliability, taste, accuracy, etc.).
Fix root causes immediately when you violate a bar (treat it like a fire).
5) Iterate with evidence: test → measure → record → improve
Principle: No guesswork—test methods and record results until they become principles (Claude Hopkins (Scientific Advertising)). Make it “better and better each year” (The Michelin Men).
Actions:
Run small experiments weekly (copy, packaging, onboarding, feature, pricing presentation).
Keep a simple “product log”: what changed, expected outcome, measured outcome, decision.
6) Use customers as contributors (tight feedback loops)
Principle: Michelin improved fast by explicitly asking users what was wrong and using that to make each edition better (The Marketing Genius of the Michelin Brothers).
Actions:
Add one persistent feedback mechanism (survey, reply-to email, call script question).
Turn top recurring feedback into the next build queue (not a “nice to have”).
7) Focus on what matters; do not let planning replace doing
Principle: Stay laser-focused on what matters (How to Make a Few Billion Dollars). Don’t let planning get in the way of doing; string incremental advances (Bloomberg by Bloomberg).
Actions:
Choose the single product bet you’ll be known for, and say no to the rest.
Operate “decisive in small steps, tentative in big direction”: act, measure, adjust frequently (Paul Graham’s Essays Part 2).
