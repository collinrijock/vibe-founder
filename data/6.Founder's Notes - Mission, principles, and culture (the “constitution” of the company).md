## Part 6) Mission, principles, and culture (the “constitution” of the company)

**Initial snippet:** A company isn’t a “candy store.” It needs an articulated mission, philosophical base, moral compass, and must keep reinventing itself ([Mind Your Own Business](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1427/?highlight=48278)).

**Question:** What do you stand for, and what behavior do you reward/punish when nobody is watching?

---

## A high-level execution system (principles + actions)

### 1) Write the “why” in plain language (mission)
- **Action:** Write a 1-paragraph mission that names the *customer* and the *standard you’re trying to set* (insurgent vibe, not vague).  
- **Action:** Make sure it’s usable by any employee to decide “what to do next” without you.  
- **Source:** Clear mission + founder influence drives responsibility and avoids bureaucracy ([John Bogle: Founder’s Mentality excerpt](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/1998/?location=01:08:05)).

### 2) Write principles that govern trade-offs (not slogans)
- **Action:** Draft 5–12 principles. Each must be a “decision rule,” not a value-word (e.g., “customer obsession beats short-term margin”).  
- **Action:** Put **service ahead of profit** in the ordering—profit matters, but it’s downstream of serving well.  
- **Sources:** Written principles keep a complicated enterprise on course ([Ogilvy on Advertising](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1614/?highlight=57601)); “principles are fundamental truths” and should be written down and shared ([Ray Dalio: Principles episode](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2034/?location=00:00:01)).

### 3) Translate principles into *behaviors you can observe*
- **Action:** For each principle, define 2 lists:
  - **We do:** 3–5 observable behaviors
  - **We don’t:** 3–5 anti-patterns (what gets you fired / not promoted)
- **Action:** Teach the *principle behind the rule* so people internalize it (not “lip service”).  
- **Source:** Explain principles behind rules; ethics + open discussion makes them real ([Principles](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1627/?highlight=60091)).

### 4) Founder time = culture formation (you can’t outsource this early)
- **Action:** In the early days, behave as the principles: do interviews, read customer emails, review outputs—people copy what you do.  
- **Action:** Repeat the same few ideas for years; consistency compounds.  
- **Sources:** Early Amazon had no formal principles because “Jeff was the principles” and did everything, transmitting philosophy by action ([Working Backwards](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1485/?highlight=51300)); founder as master communicator who teaches and repeats principles ([Working with Jeff Bezos](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2279/?location=00:06:00)).

### 5) Encode principles into mechanisms (rituals, tools, constraints)
- **Action:** Pick 1–3 mechanisms that make the culture unavoidable (meeting format, writing standard, hiring bar, spending rule).  
- **Action:** Use “shock” thoughtfully to signal difference (simple, memorable, consistent).  
- **Sources:** “Shock is a great mechanism for behavioral change” (door desks = frugality made visible) ([The Hard Thing About Hard Things](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2296/?location=00:38:11)); avoid “Day 2 stasis” by designing for continual motion ([The Space Barons](https://www.foundersnotes.com)).

### 6) Hire for mission-fit and dedication (culture is who you let in)
- **Action:** In interviews, test for genuine commitment to *your specific* mission (not perks).  
- **Action:** Protect a high bar; small teams amplify individual impact.  
- **Sources:** Cultures of extreme dedication matter ([Zero to One](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1570/?highlight=58596)); recruiting is core and mission-specific (tribe of like-minded people) ([Peter Thiel on recruiting](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2237/?location=00:49:58)).

### 7) Reinforce with storytelling + “what do we have to show for it?”
- **Action:** Keep a living doc of 10–20 “company stories” that demonstrate principles in action; retell constantly.  
- **Action:** In reviews and meetings, force concreteness: outputs over talk.  
- **Sources:** “What do we have to show for it?” as an execution demand ([The Billionaire Who Wasn't](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1552/?highlight=53703)); don’t let planning replace doing—win via many small advances ([Bloomberg by Bloomberg](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1428/?highlight=48362)).
