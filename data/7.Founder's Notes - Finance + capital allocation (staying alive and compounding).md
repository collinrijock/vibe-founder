## Part 7 — Finance + capital allocation (staying alive and compounding)

**Initial snippet:** Finance + capital allocation (staying alive and compounding): staying alive and compounding.  
**Question:** *Where does your free cash flow go—back into the business, into new bets, into acquisitions, or back to owners—and is that decision rational?*

### A high-level execution system (principles → actions)

1) **Decide what game you’re playing (durability > optics)**
- Principle: You are “unavoidably an investor,” so optimize for what will be valuable **decades** from now, not this quarter ([Peter Thiel on durability + the entrepreneur as investor](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2237/?location=00:41:58)).
- Actions:
  - Write a 1-page “capital allocation policy”: reinvestment priorities, minimum cash buffer, max leverage, and when you’d return cash to owners.

2) **Build a “don’t get knocked out” balance sheet**
- Principle: “Borrowed money must always be promptly repaid… a bad credit rating” ends careers; also guard against **overexpansion** ([J. Paul Getty’s rules](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/2334/?highlight=86745)).
- Actions:
  - Set hard limits: maximum fixed obligations you can cover under a bad-case revenue scenario.
  - If you use debt, treat repayment like a non-negotiable operating constraint.

3) **Operate with relentless economy (avoid fiscal complacency)**
- Principle: NeXT was “privileged and crippled with fiscal complacency… the money was gone” ([Steve Jobs/NeXT capital complacency](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2003/?location=00:50:01)).
- Actions:
  - Run a monthly “expense audit” (top 10 line items, owner signs off).
  - Tie every new hire/tool to a specific output you can measure within 30–90 days.

4) **Allocate capital by opportunity cost (not vibes)**
- Principle: Stay “laser-focused on what matters”; small capital can create gigantic returns if deployed into the right bets ([Brad Jacobs maxims](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1351/?highlight=43471)).
- Actions:
  - Keep a simple internal “investment memo” template for any meaningful spend: expected upside, downside, time-to-feedback, and alternatives you’re not funding.

5) **Experiment aggressively—without risking the company**
- Principle: Success = “strive for a lot and fail well… without failing bad enough to get you knocked out of the game” ([Ray Dalio on failing well](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2034/?location=00:08:08)).
- Actions:
  - Fund growth in staged tranches (small test → measured result → scale).
  - Predefine kill criteria (time/metrics) before you start spending.

6) **Don’t let “planning” replace doing—use tight feedback loops**
- Principle: “I’ve never let planning get in the way of doing… make tactical plans for the next few steps… adjust” ([Bloomberg on tactical planning](https://www.foundersnotes.com/bookreview/1428/?highlight=48362)).
- Actions:
  - Weekly cash + runway check (15 minutes).
  - Quarterly reallocation: stop 1–3 things, double down on 1–2 things.

7) **Know when *not* to sell (and when scale is worth it)**
- Principle: If you’re early in a huge market and can be #1, “remain stand-alone” because no one can pay what you’re worth ([Ben Horowitz on when not to sell](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2296/?location=00:42:11)).
- Actions:
  - Maintain an explicit “independence threshold”: the conditions under which you’d reject acquisition offers.

8) **Focus on what you can control**
- Principle: Only concern yourself with controllables; “the rest is a waste of time” ([What I Learned Before I Sold to Warren Buffett — controllables](https://www.foundersnotes.com/transcripts/2328/?location=00:28:08)).
- Actions:
  - Put controllables on a dashboard: cash, margins, receivables, churn/retention, hiring pace, customer acquisition payback.

This is the essence: **protect the downside (survive), avoid forced growth, and deploy capital into the few highest-ROI bets with fast feedback—so you can keep compounding.**
